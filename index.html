The generation of high quality chronologies is a consistent challenge in the paleogeosciences. Understanding paleoclimatic change, sediment budgets, glacial advances, volcanic processes, and biodiversity change through time all rely on high quality chronologies. For each of these applications disciplinary groups within the geosciences have arisen, each with different data sharing & archiving practices, and each with different vocabularies.

The National Science Foundation in the United States has recognized that similar issues exist across the geosciences, and that technological advances in cyberinfrastructure and super computing can provide solutions to these problems. EarthCube, one such initiative, has brought together geoscientists, data scientists and computer scientists to address such issues. By addressing challenges at an institutional level through directed funding solicitations and at a scientific level through individual projects, EarthCube helps to overcome many of the barriers to interdisciplinary data science.

This presentation provides an overview of EarthCube, and also three specific use cases relevant to the geosciences, where EarthCube funded technology acts to minimize chronological uncertainty in the paleogeosciences, harvests "dark data" from the publication record, and a newly funded project that will link geochronological data, from lab to publication, to enable dynamic scientific reports that update as labs re-calibrate instrumentation, or new data becomes available.

EarthCube was initiated by the National Science Foundation (NSF) in 2011, providing the fist block of funding in 2013. EarthCube aims to transform geoscience research by developing cyberinfrastructure to improve access, sharing, visualization, and analysis of all forms of geosciences data and related resources. As a community-governed effort, EarthCube's goal is to enable geoscientists to tackle the challenges of understanding and predicting a complex and evolving solid Earth, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and space environment systems. The NSF's Directorate for Geosciences (GEO) and the Division of Advanced Cyberinfrastructure (ACI) partnered to sponsor EarthCube, which NSF anticipates supporting through 2022.
